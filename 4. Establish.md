# Chapter 4 - Establish: From New Birth to New Formation

If Chapter 3 asked how the church moves toward people, Chapter 4 asks what the church must do after people respond. Engage brings people to the doorstep. It meets urgent needs so the gospel can address the ultimate need. A heart opens. The Spirit draws. Repentance begins. Faith awakens. A person receives Christ, perhaps through a miracle, a meal, a conversation, a crisis, a kindness, or a long friendship that finally becomes clear. No matter how it began, something decisive has happened. They have started.

But starting is not the same thing as being formed. Conversion is a miracle. Formation is a process. Engage brings people to Christ; Establish forms the character of Christ in people. The goal of Engage is encounter. The goal of Establish is formation. It is the slow, deep, durable shaping of a life that begins to think, desire, obey, endure, repent, forgive, and love like Jesus. Without that work, new believers remain spiritually vulnerable even after they have sincerely responded to the gospel.

That matters because nobody comes to Jesus in a neutral environment. New believers do not step out of the waters of repentance into an empty field. They step back into homes, friendships, screens, habits, ideologies, appetites, fears, and rival stories that have already been forming them for years. The principalities do not stop contending for the human person simply because a first yes has been spoken to Christ. Media keeps discipling. Philosophies keep whispering. Algorithms keep catechizing. Old reflexes keep resurfacing. So the question becomes urgent: how does a new believer avoid being pulled back into the same pattern that shaped them before?

Romans 12 answers with a command that is both simple and searching: do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind. That command implies two things at once. First, there is always a pattern pressing in from the outside. Second, real transformation is possible. But it also forces a harder question. If only God can truly change a person, why does He not simply transform us all at once?

## The Battle After Beginning

This is where many churches become careless. We celebrate the moment of response, but we do not always reckon honestly with the battle that follows it. Jesus Himself warned us through the parable of the sower that the same seed can meet very different kinds of soil. Some hear the word and never receive it deeply. Some receive it with joy, but because they have no root, they fall away when pressure comes. Others begin well, but the cares of the world, the deceitfulness of riches, and competing desires choke the word until it becomes unfruitful. The point is not that the seed is weak. The point is that formation matters.

Engage scatters the seed. Establish helps the roots go down. Without rootedness, a person may have real emotion, real sincerity, and even real initial movement toward Christ, yet still remain vulnerable to being choked by the same forces that shaped them before. That is not only a first-century problem. It is a modern one as well. A person may come alive to Jesus on Sunday and return Monday to a workplace shaped by cynicism, a campus shaped by suspicion, a phone shaped by vanity, a friend group shaped by appetite, and a family system shaped by confusion. If the church does not help them build new patterns of life, the old pattern will not politely step aside.

This is why Establish cannot be treated as an optional extra for especially serious Christians. It is part of obedience to the Great Commission. Jesus did not say merely to make converts. He said to make disciples, teaching them to obey all that He commanded. That means the church is responsible not only to announce Christ, but to help people learn His ways. We do not control conversion, and we do not manufacture holiness, but we are commanded to participate in the work of formation.

The early church understood this instinctively. When people believed, they were not congratulated and then abandoned to figure out the Christian life alone. They were brought into a people. They were taught. They were prayed for. They were known. They were corrected. They were sustained. They were established.

## How God Changes People

We cannot change ourselves at the deepest level. Willpower can modify behavior for a while, fear can produce outward compliance, and religious pressure can create a temporary appearance of change, but none of those things can give new life. Only God can do that. Only God can open blind eyes, soften the heart, convict of sin, awaken love for holiness, and write His law where it was once resisted. That is why conversion is a miracle, and why sanctification is never merely self-improvement with Bible verses attached.

But if only God can change us, why does He not simply complete the whole work instantly? Part of the answer is that God does not save people as machines. He forms sons and daughters. He does not merely erase guilt. He teaches obedience. He does not simply remove old patterns by force. He renews the mind, trains the affections, redirects desire, and teaches His people to walk with Him. His transformations are supernatural, but they are not impersonal.

Scripture holds both sides together. God is the One who works in His people, and His people are called to respond in obedient participation. We are saved by grace. We are being saved as grace works its way deeper into the whole of life. We will be saved fully when Christ completes what He began. In the meantime, God ordinarily forms His people through means He has appointed: truth received, sin confessed, prayer practiced, habits reordered, burdens shared, and obedience learned in real time.

That is why Romans 12 does not say, "Wait passively until change arrives." It says, "Be transformed by the renewal of your mind." God is the One who transforms, but He does so as the mind is renewed under His truth. Jesus prays, "Sanctify them in the truth; Your word is truth." Paul says that all Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, reproof, correction, and training in righteousness. God changes people, but He does not usually do so apart from His Word, apart from His Spirit, or apart from His people.

The church's role, then, is not to pretend it can manufacture life. Its role is to steward the environment where life grows. Farmers do not create life, but they still till soil, remove weeds, water faithfully, and protect what has been planted. In the same way, the church cannot create spiritual life, but it is still commanded to teach, pray, correct, encourage, carry, and walk with believers until the truth takes root. Only God gives growth, but He is pleased to give growth through an obedient church.

## The Three Streams of Establish

Christian formation is not vague, and it is not accidental. Again and again, Scripture shows God using three streams to mature His people: the Word of God, the Holy Spirit, and fellowship. Remove any one of these, and growth becomes unstable. People may still look active for a time, but faith becomes thin, hidden sin remains hidden, prayer becomes occasional, and belief settles into information rather than transformation.

The Word of God establishes believers by renewing the mind. A new believer cannot withstand a hostile age with borrowed phrases and secondhand convictions. They need Scripture not as a religious accessory, but as living truth. The Word teaches, corrects, exposes, washes, and anchors. It gives names to sin, substance to hope, clarity to confusion, and shape to obedience. This is why establishing someone in Christ includes opening the Bible with them, teaching them the storyline of creation, fall, redemption, and restoration, and helping them obey what they read rather than merely admire it. The goal is not to produce people who know Christian vocabulary. It is to produce people whose inner life is being re-ordered by truth.

The Holy Spirit establishes believers by making that truth living and personal. The Spirit is not an optional add-on to Christian maturity. He comforts the weary, convicts the compromised, counsels the confused, and strengthens the weak for obedience. He teaches. He reminds. He guides into truth. He produces fruit that no church program can manufacture: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. Establish, therefore, is not behavior management. It is Spirit-empowered formation. New believers must be taught not only how to read Scripture, but how to pray with dependence, respond to conviction, discern between condemnation and correction, and walk with God rather than merely think about Him.

Fellowship establishes believers by refusing isolation. Many believers drift not because they lack access to information, but because they try to survive spiritually alone. That is dangerous, especially in a culture where rival forms of discipleship are continuous. God did not design Christian formation as private spirituality. He designed it as shared life. In Acts, believers devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching, the fellowship, the breaking of bread, and the prayers. Those four anchors belong together. Teaching without fellowship can become abstraction. Fellowship without truth becomes sentiment. Prayer without community becomes unstable. But when the Word, the Spirit, and fellowship converge, something steady happens. People begin to love God more deeply, desire holiness more honestly, and grow into a life that is set apart without becoming theatrical.

This is why table life matters so much. Shared meals are not sentimental details in the New Testament. They are one of the ordinary places where belonging becomes believable. Around tables people slow down, speak honestly, confess weakness, ask questions, and learn to carry one another. Fellowship, in the biblical sense, is not socializing with a Christian accent. It is koinonia. It is shared responsibility in Christ. It is belonging with accountability, tenderness with truth, grace with growth.

## The Pattern of Acts

If we want to know what Establish looks like in practice, the Book of Acts gives us the blueprint. After people were saved, they did not simply go home to hostile culture with a handshake and a pamphlet. They were joined to a people who stayed together. They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching, to fellowship, to the breaking of bread, and to prayer. They shared life. They shared burdens. They shared resources. They learned to worship, repent, endure, and witness in community.

That mattered because the surrounding world was not neutral. The first Christians went back into cities full of idols, pressure, immorality, fear, empire, and social cost. Yet the church did not respond by reducing formation to private inspiration. It created a communal life strong enough to hold people steady in a hostile environment. That is still the need today. New believers are not returning to pagan temples, perhaps, but they are returning to hostile habits, hostile messages, hostile desires, and hostile social pressures. If the church does not offer a real counter-formation, the world will continue its work uninterrupted.

In contemporary church life, that kind of proximity often takes shape through small groups, home groups, mentoring friendships, and one-to-one discipleship. Large gatherings can proclaim. They can inspire. They can clarify. But formation requires proximity. People need spaces where they are seen, known, corrected gently, and carried patiently. They need places where confession is normal, prayer is practiced, and the "one another" commands of the New Testament become visible life instead of admired ideals. Bear one another's burdens. Confess your sins to one another and pray for one another. Forgive one another as God in Christ forgave you. Those commands require more than attendance. They require fellowship that is real enough to expose what isolation hides.

That also means the church must guard against counterfeit fellowship. A small group can become a performance just as easily as a platform can. If leaders pretend to be perfect, new believers learn to hide. If pride rules the room, honesty dies. If confidentiality is weak, trust collapses. If correction comes without tenderness, bruised people withdraw. Establish must never feel like, "Get your act together." The Messiah does not break a bruised reed or quench a faintly burning wick. His way is patient, truthful, gentle, and serious all at once. So the culture of Establish must be marked by humility, confidentiality, prayer, confession, forgiveness, and non-crushing truth.

## The Aim of Establish

The goal of Establish is not to produce religious people who know Bible facts and can speak the right language. The goal is formation. It is character. It is to see people rooted deeply enough in Christ that they begin to fear God more than man, love holiness more than self-display, and obey Jesus more steadily than their emotions. It is to see minds renewed, desires re-ordered, habits retrained, wounds brought into the light, and relationships slowly healed under the rule of Christ.

This is why the church must not stop with engagement. To introduce people to Jesus and then leave them under-formed is to leave them exposed. We are being saved, and God is truly transforming His people, but disciples are also commanded to obey Christ by discipling others. The church is called not only to engage the lost, but to establish the found. It is called to walk with believers until the truth settles into their bones and the life of Christ begins to take recognizable shape.

When Establish is happening well, people become less easily choked. They do not become perfect, but they become rooted. They know where to go when temptation rises. They know how to repent without despair. They learn the difference between conviction and condemnation. They begin to pray instead of merely panic. They learn to confess instead of hide. They discover that obedience is not the enemy of freedom, but one of its first fruits.

And once people are rooted, the church must not keep them in spiritual infancy. Establish is not an endless waiting room. It is preparation for useful, durable, Spirit-shaped life in the body of Christ and in the world. Engage brings people to Jesus. Establish roots them in Jesus. The next movement will ask how rooted believers are then released to serve, witness, and multiply without skipping the work of formation that makes such release trustworthy.
