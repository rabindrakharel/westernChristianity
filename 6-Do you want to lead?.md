# Chapter 6 - Do You Want to Lead?

If Chapter 5 asked whether the church will release believers into ministry, Chapter 6 asks what kind of people should lead them. That question matters because once a church begins to empower people, leadership becomes even more important, not less. A multiplying church does not need more performers. It needs shepherds.

So the question is not first whether you want influence, visibility, or a meaningful role. The question is simpler and heavier than that: do you want to lead? And if you do, do you understand what biblical leadership actually is?

The whole book has prepared us for this moment. Chapter 1 asked who you are. Chapter 2 asked what you are called to do. Chapters 3 through 5 traced how that calling moves outward, takes root, and multiplies. Leadership exists to protect that whole movement. Leaders help people know who they are in Christ, live with mission, engage the searching, establish the new believer, and release others into faithful service.

## Shepherds, Not Performers

When Jesus restored Peter, He did not tell him to build a platform. He told him, "Feed My sheep." That remains one of the clearest leadership commissions in the New Testament because it is so concrete. Feed them. Tend them. Care for them.

Performers ask how they are being perceived. Shepherds ask how the people are actually doing. Performers manage energy in a room. Shepherds notice drift in a soul. Performers can be satisfied with applause. Shepherds are not satisfied until people are being fed, protected, corrected, and strengthened.

This is where much modern leadership becomes disordered. Some leaders know how to gather attention, but they do not know how to carry people. They can run meetings, but they do not walk patiently with weakness. They can build momentum, but they do not always build saints. The church does not need less giftedness. It needs giftedness submitted to shepherding love.

To lead with this book's framework in view means keeping Christ, character, and calling in front of people at all times. Leaders help the lost move toward Christ. They help believers grow in character. They keep calling visible so the church does not drift back into spectatorship.

## Leaders Under the Word and the Spirit

No one is fit to lead who is unwilling to remain led. Leaders are not above Scripture. They are under it. They are not sustained by gifting alone. They are sustained by truth, prayer, repentance, and the help of the Holy Spirit.

If you want to lead, you must live in the Word not as a professional resource, but as daily bread. You must pray with honesty. You must ask the Holy Spirit for help because you actually need Him. A leader who stops listening to God soon starts leading out of instinct, insecurity, or habit. A leader who stops reading Scripture as a disciple begins using Scripture merely as a tool.

This is why Paul tells elders to pay careful attention to themselves and to all the flock. The order matters. A leader must watch his own life, because dry souls eventually produce harsh, evasive, manipulative, or performative leadership. Leaders who are being renewed gain patience, gravity, humility, and truthfulness.

Servant leadership is not weakness. It is strength governed by love. It tells the truth, but not to humiliate. It corrects, but not to crush. It protects, but not to control. It serves, but not to be admired for serving.

## Authentic and Accountable

If you want to lead, do not pretend to be perfect. You are not. The church is not helped by leaders who create the illusion of flawlessness. It is harmed by them. When leaders perform perfection, people learn to hide. When leaders act untouchable, honesty dies around them.

Authenticity does not mean carelessness or making every private struggle public. It means truthfulness. It means you can say, when appropriate, "I was wrong." "I need prayer." "I need help." "I need the grace of God too." That kind of honesty does not weaken leadership. It keeps leadership human and keeps repentance normal.

No leader should try to carry authority alone. The New Testament pattern is not lone spiritual heroes, but shared oversight, mutual strengthening, and accountable leadership. If you want to lead, let other leaders ask hard questions about your doctrine, your marriage, your use of money, your ambition, your sexuality, your speech, your fatigue, and your patterns of secrecy. A leader without accountability is not freer. He is more dangerous.

Accountability protects the leader, the church, and the gospel. Many leadership failures do not begin in public rebellion. They begin in private concealment. Hidden grievance becomes bitterness. Fatigue becomes numbness. Praise becomes entitlement. Untested authority becomes self-importance. Accountability interrupts that decay early.

## Leading for Multiplication

Biblical leadership is never an end in itself. The point of leading is not to remain needed forever. It is to make the body stronger, the mission clearer, and the people more fruitful.

That means leaders must build contexts where Engage, Establish, and Empower can actually happen. They must make room for tables, prayer, confession, shared burdens, Scripture, hospitality, and visible pathways of responsibility. Larger gatherings may still matter for worship, teaching, testimony, and wider commissioning. But wise leaders refuse to treat the stage as the unquestioned center of church life, because people are formed most deeply where they can actually be seen, challenged, and entrusted.

In a multiplying church, recognized leaders do not hoard ministry. They keep equipping more people to host, pray, teach faithfully, care for others, and carry spiritual responsibility. That is how leadership outlives personality. If a church lost its building, its central figure, or its usual production, would the ministry of Christ still continue through the people? Healthy leaders labor for a yes.

The same principle applies to resources. Budgets should serve mission, formation, mercy, and sending. Buildings and production may sometimes assist that work, but they can never become the work itself. A leader who cannot distinguish between the mission and the machinery will eventually protect the wrong thing.

## Here Am I

By this point, the whole book gathers into one call. Know who you are in Christ. Recover what you are called to do. Engage people with mercy and truth. Establish believers until roots go deep. Empower the saints for the work of ministry. And if God entrusts you with leadership, lead as a servant who can say yes to that whole burden.

Isaiah gives us the right order. He sees the holiness of God, becomes aware of his own uncleanness, receives cleansing from the Lord, and then hears the question: "Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?" Only then does he answer, "Here am I. Send me." Biblical leadership does not begin with ambition. It begins with encounter, repentance, grace, and surrender.

The people around us are still carrying the ache of Chapter 1. They are still asking whether life has meaning, whether anyone can tell them who they are, and whether there is a purpose big enough to hold their pain, failure, and longing. They do not need another personality cult. They do not need a church obsessed with its own preservation. They need leaders who will feed the sheep, equip the saints, and send laborers into the harvest.

And yes, this is how light returns. Not mainly through spectacle, outrage, or the fantasy that one gifted figure can reverse what years of spiritual neglect have damaged. Light returns when ordinary people are taught to obey Christ in the ordinary places where life is actually lived. Homes are reordered. Children are raised in truth and tenderness. Men and women alike learn courage, honesty, repentance, self-control, and forgiveness. Neighbors are welcomed. Burdens are shared. Sin is confessed. Christ is named.

Renewal will never be manufactured by technique, and no generation will eradicate sin before the Lord returns. But real renewal is still possible. Where Christ is obeyed, the vandalism of sin does not get the final word. One life changes. Then a family. Then a table. Then a street. This is how the witness of the kingdom becomes visible again.

So let the call remain concrete. Make disciples. Teach obedience. Open your home. Pray with people. Tell the truth. Feed the sheep. Train younger believers patiently. Release others into ministry. If you want to lead, lead there. That is where purpose becomes visible again. That is where the church becomes useful again. That is where a damaged world begins to feel the healing pressure of a people who really belong to Jesus.
