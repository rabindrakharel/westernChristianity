# Chapter 6 - Do You Want to Lead?

If Chapter 5 asked whether the church will release believers into ministry, Chapter 6 asks what kind of people should lead them. That question matters because once a church begins to empower people, leadership becomes even more important, not less. A multiplying church does not need more performers. It needs shepherds. It needs men and women who can love people, feed people, protect people, correct people, pray for people, and keep the mission of Christ clear in the middle of real weakness.

So the question is not first whether you want influence, visibility, or a meaningful role. The question is simpler and heavier than that: do you want to lead? And if you do, do you understand what biblical leadership actually is?

In the kingdom of God, leadership is not self-exaltation. It is service. Jesus did not define greatness by platform, charisma, or control. He knelt down and washed feet. He told His disciples that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over others, but that it must not be so among them. Whoever would be great must become a servant. Whoever would be first must become a slave. Christian leadership begins there or it does not begin at all.

That is why leadership in the church must always stay tied to the same mission we have already traced through this book. The goal of Engage is Christ. The goal of Establish is character. The goal of leadership is to help people move toward both without losing either. A leader helps people meet Christ and become like Christ. A leader does not gather people around himself. He gathers people around Jesus and keeps them there.

## Shepherds, Not Performers

When Jesus restored Peter, He did not tell him to build a brand. He told him, "Feed My sheep." That is one of the clearest leadership commissions in the New Testament, and it remains searching because it is so ordinary. Feed them. Tend them. Care for them. Leadership is not first about vision statements or public presence. It is about people who belong to Christ and need to be nourished, guarded, and guided.

That means a leader must learn to think like a shepherd rather than a performer. Performers ask how they are being perceived. Shepherds ask how the people are actually doing. Performers manage energy in a room. Shepherds notice drift in a soul. Performers crave response. Shepherds endure hidden labor. Performers can be satisfied with applause. Shepherds are not satisfied until people are being fed.

This is where a great deal of modern church leadership becomes disordered. Some leaders know how to gather attention, but they do not know how to carry people. They can speak publicly, but they do not walk patiently with weakness. They can run meetings, but they do not form households. They can build momentum, but they do not always build saints. The church does not need less giftedness. It needs giftedness submitted to shepherding love.

To lead with Engage in mind means you do not forget the lost. You remember that people around your church, around your table, and around your people still need Christ. You teach your church to move outward. You pray for the harvest. You keep the gospel clear. To lead with Establish in mind means you do not stop at attendance. You care about formation, repentance, holiness, healed relationships, deep roots in the Word, and humble responsiveness to the Spirit. Leadership must hold both together. Christ as the goal. Character as the fruit.

## Lead as a Servant Under the Word

No one is fit to lead who is unwilling to remain led. Leaders are not above Scripture. They are under it. They are not sustained by gifting alone. They are sustained by truth, prayer, and the help of the Holy Spirit. If you want to lead, you must live in the Word not as a professional resource, but as daily bread. You must read with dependence, pray with honesty, and ask the Holy Spirit for help not as a formality, but because you actually need Him.

That need does not disappear as leadership responsibility grows. It intensifies. A leader who stops listening to God soon starts leading out of instinct, insecurity, or habit. A leader who stops reading Scripture as a disciple begins using Scripture merely as a tool. A leader who stops asking for the Spirit's help may still sound competent for a while, but eventually the inner life grows thin, and thin leaders cannot nourish others for long.

This is why Paul tells elders to pay careful attention to themselves and to all the flock. The order matters. A leader must watch his own life, not because leadership is self-obsession, but because unexamined leaders eventually wound the people they are called to serve. If your soul is dry, your leadership will become harsh, performative, evasive, or manipulative. If your inner life is being renewed, your leadership gains gravity, patience, and truthfulness.

Servant leadership is not weakness. It is strength governed by love. It tells the truth, but not to humiliate. It corrects, but not to crush. It protects, but not to control. It serves, but not to be admired for serving. This is one reason Peter tells elders to shepherd the flock of God not under compulsion, not for shameful gain, and not domineering over those in their charge, but being examples to the flock. The leader does not stand over the people as an untouchable figure. He walks before them as one who must also obey.

## Authentic, Not Untouchable

If you want to lead, do not pretend to be perfect. You are not. The church is not helped by leaders who create the illusion of flawlessness. It is harmed by them. When leaders perform perfection, people learn to hide. When leaders act untouchable, honesty dies around them. When leaders cannot admit weakness, confession becomes dangerous for everyone else too.

Authenticity does not mean carelessness, emotional exhibitionism, or making every private struggle public. It means truthfulness. It means you do not build ministry on a false image. It means you can say, when appropriate, "I was wrong." "I need prayer." "I am tired." "I need help." "I need the grace of God too." That kind of honesty does not weaken leadership. It humanizes it and keeps it from becoming theatrical.

This matters because leadership is deeply imitative. People rarely become more honest than the culture their leaders permit. If leaders hide, people hide. If leaders confess carefully and repent concretely, people learn that repentance is normal. If leaders practice forgiveness rather than merely teaching it, churches become safer places to heal. If leaders insist on appearing strong at all times, everyone else learns to protect themselves the same way.

To lead well, then, is to walk in repentance openly enough that others can follow you into the light. It is to practice forgiveness, not just preach it. It is to admit weakness without making weakness an excuse. It is to ask for prayer because you know leadership does not remove your need for grace. The church does not need invulnerable leaders. It needs truthful ones.

## Accountable Leadership

No leader should try to carry authority alone. The New Testament pattern is not lone spiritual heroes, but shared oversight, mutual strengthening, and accountable leadership. Elders are plural. Workers are sent with companions. Churches pray together, discern together, and correct one another. Even the apostolic ministry we see in Acts is not a story of solitary men answerable to no one. It is a story of recognized leaders functioning within the life of the body.

That is still essential now. If you want to lead, walk in accountability with other leaders. Let people ask hard questions about your doctrine, your marriage, your use of money, your emotional life, your sexuality, your ambition, your fatigue, and your patterns of speech. Do not build a life in which nobody can tell you the truth. A leader without accountability is not freer. He is more dangerous.

Accountability is not suspicion. It is protection. It protects the leader from drift, the church from preventable damage, and the gospel from unnecessary scandal. It also protects the soul of the leader from isolation. Many leadership failures do not begin in public rebellion. They begin in private concealment. A hidden grievance becomes bitterness. Fatigue becomes numbness. Praise becomes entitlement. Untested authority becomes self-importance. Accountability interrupts that decay early.

This is one reason leadership in a healthy church should never function like a private kingdom. Decisions should not be hidden behind spiritual mystique. Correction should not be treated as betrayal. Financial handling should not be opaque. No leader should be surrounded only by admirers. If that culture is allowed to form, leadership slowly stops looking like shepherding and starts looking like domination. And once domination enters, Engage weakens, Establish becomes distorted, and Empower collapses.

## Leading for Multiplication

Biblical leadership is never an end in itself. The point of leading is not to remain needed forever. It is to make the body stronger, the mission clearer, and the people more fruitful. That means leadership must always be measured by whether it helps others engage and establish. Are people being taught to reach the lost? Are they being formed in character? Are new leaders emerging? Are households becoming stronger? Are more people learning to serve, disciple, pray, host, reconcile, and carry burdens?

This is where many churches again reveal what they truly value. Some leaders do not mind people being helped, as long as those people never become strong enough to lead alongside them. But that is not kingdom leadership. Kingdom leadership rejoices when others grow, when others carry responsibility well, and when others begin to bear real fruit. John the Baptist captured the posture in a single line: He must increase, but I must decrease. Every church leader must learn to pray that without pretending.

If leadership is going to multiply rather than merely impress, it must take visible shape. The simplest way to say it is this: Engage, Establish, Empower. Engage keeps Christ in view for the lost. Establish keeps character in view for the found. Empower turns formed believers into laborers who can now help others engage and establish. A healthy church does not merely teach these words. It builds its life around them.

Leaders must therefore build contexts where that pattern can actually happen. In a multiplying church, much of the weekly texture of life will be relational and participatory: homes, tables, prayer, confession, Scripture, and shared burdens. This is where neighbors can be invited naturally, where new believers can be established patiently, and where emerging leaders can begin serving before they are polished. Larger corporate gatherings may still have an important place for worship, testimony, doctrine, and wider commissioning. But wise leaders resist making the stage the unquestioned center of church life, because people are formed most deeply where they can actually be seen, challenged, and entrusted.

In that kind of pattern, recognized leaders do not hoard ministry. They keep equipping more people to host, pray, teach faithfully, and care for others. That is how apostolic leadership functions in the New Testament. It keeps doctrine clear, character serious, and mission moving, while more and more believers begin carrying real responsibility. The result is not chaos. It is distributed faithfulness under real spiritual oversight.

The same principle applies to resources. Budgets should serve mission, formation, mercy, and sending. Buildings and production may sometimes assist that work, but they can never become the work itself. A leader who cannot distinguish between the mission and the machinery will eventually protect the wrong thing.

This is why the leadership question becomes so practical: if a church lost its building, its central personality, or its usual production, would the ministry of Christ still continue through the people? Healthy leaders labor for a yes. They feed people, form people, and send people in ways that outlive their own visibility.

If you want to lead, then lead in a way that leaves behind more leaders, not more dependents. Lead so that the church can still be healthy if you are absent. Lead so that people grow in Christ, not merely in attachment to your personality. Lead so that your community becomes more honest, more prayerful, more scriptural, more forgiving, and more outward-facing because you were there. Lead so that the mission outlives your role.

That is how servant leadership becomes fruitful multiplication. It begins with kneeling, not posturing. It stays close to the Word and the Spirit. It walks in repentance and accountability. It refuses the lie of perfection. And it keeps feeding the sheep with Christ in view, character in view, and the harvest still in view.

## Here Am I

By this point, the book has come full circle. Chapter 1 named a broken world: families under strain, young people starved for meaning, truth thinned out, loneliness spreading, and churches busy while society weakens. That world is still here at the end of Chapter 6. The confusion has not vanished. The need has not diminished. If anything, the question has become more personal.

Isaiah shows us the shape of a true calling. He sees the holiness of God, becomes painfully aware of his own uncleanness, receives cleansing from the Lord, and then hears the divine question: "Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?" Only then does he answer, "Here am I. Send me." That order matters. Vision of God. Truth about self. Cleansing by grace. Then sending. Biblical leadership does not begin with ambition. It begins with encounter, repentance, and surrender.

The New Testament presses the same burden outward. How will people call on the One they have not believed in? How will they believe in the One they have not heard? And how will they hear without someone proclaiming Him? The broken world of Chapter 1 is filled with people who are still waiting to hear. They do not need another monument. They do not need another personality cult. They do not need a church preoccupied with its own preservation. They need the gospel. They need households full of prayer. They need leaders who will feed the sheep, equip the saints, and send laborers into the harvest.

So this is where the chapters gather into one call. Know who you are. Recover what you are called to do. Engage the world with Christ in view. Establish believers until character takes root. Empower the saints for the work of ministry. And if God entrusts you with leadership, lead as a servant who can say yes to that whole burden.

Do you want to lead? Then do not ask first for a platform. Ask for clean hands, a truthful heart, courage under Scripture, help from the Holy Spirit, and brothers and sisters who will keep you accountable. Ask for love strong enough to serve without posturing. Ask for faith deep enough to release others rather than control them. Ask for grace to say what Isaiah said: Here am I. Send me.

And yes, this is how light returns. Not through spectacle, and not through the fantasy that one election, one institution, or one gifted leader can reverse a civilizational collapse that discipleship neglect helped create. It returns when ordinary people are taught to obey Christ in the ordinary places where a society either heals or decays. Homes are reordered. Marriages grow more faithful. Children are raised in truth and tenderness. Men and women alike learn courage, self-control, honesty, and forgiveness. Neighbors are welcomed. Possessions are held more loosely. Speech grows cleaner. Work grows more honest. Burdens are shared. Sin is confessed. This is not sentimental. It is social architecture.

Societal degradation is not solved mainly by better branding, louder outrage, or occasional moral panic. Laws can restrain some evil. Institutions can preserve some order. But they cannot create holiness, teach repentance, or make a people love what is good. The gospel reaches deeper. The Word of God renews the mind. The Spirit of God reshapes desires. The people of God provide a visible pattern of shared life. That is how the rule of Christ becomes visible again in a darkening age.

We should be careful here. The church does not manufacture paradise by technique, and no generation will eradicate sin before the Lord returns. But real renewal is still possible. Where Christ is obeyed, the vandalism of sin does not get the final word. Few gifts are greater to give your children than a living inheritance of truth, repentance, prayer, courage, and faithful example. A faithful church can make a neighborhood less lonely, a marriage less brittle, a child less abandoned, and a community more honest about what is true and good. One life changes. Then a family. Then a table. Then a street. This is how the witness of the kingdom becomes visible again.

So let the call remain concrete. Make disciples. Teach obedience. Open your home. Pray with people. Tell the truth. Feed the sheep. Raise children in the fear of the Lord. Train younger believers patiently. Release others into ministry. If you want to lead, lead there. That is where light becomes public again. That is where a damaged society begins, however slowly, to feel the healing pressure of a people who really belong to Jesus.
