# Chapter 6 - Leadership

A multiplying church does not only need good intentions. It needs leaders who make the path clear. It is easy for a leader to say, "I am not stopping anyone from making disciples. I cannot force people." That sounds humble, but it can hide passivity. Leadership is not only about refusing to block good work. Leadership is about building a church life where people actually know how to engage, establish, and empower others.

If the pathway is foggy, most people will not suddenly discover it on their own. They will attend, consume, volunteer, and stay generally positive, but they will not know how to take responsibility for souls. They need more than permission. They need clarity, proximity, example, correction, and repeated practice.

## Leadership Must Make the Path Clear

Biblical leadership is not vague goodwill. Jesus told Peter, "Feed My sheep." Paul told the Ephesian elders to watch themselves and the flock. Leaders do not merely preach ideals. They build patterns of life that help people obey Christ in real time.

That is why framework matters. A church that says disciple-making is important but provides no usable pattern will usually drift into activity without formation. People will hear the value of mission and still not know what to do on Tuesday night, at a hospital bedside, around a kitchen table, or after a promising first contact at church.

A clear framework does not replace the Holy Spirit. It gives people a faithful shape they can actually use. In this book, that shape is simple: we engage, we establish, and we empower.

Engage moves toward people with mercy, truth, and gospel presence. Establish roots them in Word, Spirit, and fellowship until new life in Christ becomes durable. That is where much of the real equipping happens. People are taught, corrected, encouraged, and trained close enough to become durable. Empower entrusts real responsibility so formed believers can help establish and empower others in turn. When leaders keep that pathway visible, the church stops guessing.

## Proximity Is Not Optional

Discipleship needs proximity. Without proximity, you may inspire people, gather people, and even impress people. But you will not establish them, and you will not truly empower them.

Establishing someone means you know enough to see where he is drifting, where he is hiding, what questions he is carrying, what sins he is fighting, and what grace he needs to keep going. Empowering someone means you trust him with real responsibility while staying close enough to correct, steady, and strengthen him.

That cannot happen only from a stage. It happens at tables, in cars, after gatherings, during follow-up calls, in hospital rooms, on walks, in prayer, and in repeated ordinary presence. If a younger believer only hears you teach from a distance, he may learn your vocabulary without learning your way. He needs to watch you do it, and then do it with you.

## Paul and Timothy Show the Pattern

Timothy did not learn ministry from a slogan. He learned it by walking near Paul. He saw Paul's teaching, conduct, aim in life, faith, patience, love, and endurance. He watched doctrine embodied in a real man under real pressure.

Then Paul tells him something even more important. What you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses, entrust to faithful men who will be able to teach others also. That is four generations in one verse: Paul, Timothy, faithful men, others also.

That is not accidental. It is the pattern. Mature leaders bring younger men close. They teach them truth. They let them see how ministry actually happens. They entrust them with real work before they feel impressive. They correct them, steady them, and send them. Paul did not keep Timothy in a permanent category called promising. He brought him near, and he put him to work. If leaders never do that, younger men will know how to admire ministry. They will not know how to carry it.

So do not wait until a younger believer looks finished. If his character is real and his heart is teachable, establish him and empower him at the same time. Let him watch you visit the sick. Let him hear how you pray with a struggling couple. Let him help you follow up a new believer. Let him open Scripture in your living room. Then talk afterward about what went well, what went poorly, and what faithfulness looks like next time. "Watch me do it" must become "Do it with me" and then "You do it too."

## The Framework Leaders Must Protect

Leaders often say, "I am not restricting anyone." But if there is no clear pathway, no close training, and no real entrusting, then people are still being restricted in practice. They are being left to guess.

A healthy leader keeps asking:

- Who are we engaging?
- How are we establishing those who respond?
- Who are we empowering next?
- Who is close enough to learn by watching?
- What is the next obedient step after first contact, first confession, first healing, first interest, or first willingness?

If a church cannot answer those questions, it is not yet leading clearly. It may be sincere. It may be active. It may even be growing. But it is not yet forming a people who can reproduce the life of Christ in others.

## What This Looks Like in Church Life

A leader should be able to point at a ministry and say exactly what Engage, Establish, and Empower look like there.

### Music Class

Engage begins when children and parents meet patient Christians and want to come back.

Establish begins when those families are known by name, invited into prayer, Scripture, meals, and shared life beyond the class.

Empower begins when older students help younger ones, families welcome new families, and some begin serving and carrying responsibility with joy.

### Seniors Game Night

Engage begins when isolated older adults find friendship, laughter, and safety.

Establish begins when leaders know their griefs, health fears, and spiritual questions, pray with them, open the Word, visit them, and bring them into durable fellowship.

Empower begins when those seniors are no longer treated only as recipients. They begin praying for younger believers, calling the lonely, hosting, encouraging, and becoming spiritual mothers and fathers in the church.

### Healing on a Missions Trip

Engage may happen in a moment when mercy and power make Christ visible. But healing is not the finish line.

Establish begins with follow-up, teaching, repentance, prayer, local church connection, and a real community that helps the person walk with Jesus after the moment.

Empower begins when the healed person learns to testify, pray for others, serve faithfully, and become part of the church's life instead of remaining only a story from the trip.

### A Seminar at a University

Engage begins when honest questions are opened in public.

Establish begins when interested students move from auditorium to table, from event to repeated conversation, from curiosity to Scripture, prayer, confession, and accountable friendship.

Empower begins when those same students host the next discussion, welcome skeptical friends, lead Bible study in residence, and help younger students follow Christ.

### High School Pizza and Game Night

Engage begins when teenagers feel welcome enough to keep coming back.

Establish begins when adults and older students know their names, ask about the real week, follow up on hidden battles, open the Bible, pray, and stay present when life gets messy.

Empower begins when older teens welcome new students, share testimony, lead prayer, host small groups, and learn to bring friends into the same life that is forming them.

### Open Gym or Sports Night

Engage begins with honest relationship in a place where some men will actually show up.

Establish begins when one or two leaders stay long enough to ask deeper questions, meet outside the gym, read Scripture, pray, and address anger, lust, pride, fatherhood, money, and faith.

Empower begins when those same men gather others, pray before the game, check on absent friends, and learn to shepherd younger men in ordinary places.

### A Meal for Young Mothers

Engage begins when exhausted women find relief, welcome, and help.

Establish begins when someone stays after the meal, listens to hidden fear or shame, opens Scripture, prays, and keeps returning.

Empower begins when those same women carry younger mothers, organize care, lead prayer, open homes, and speak hope out of what Christ has done in them.

That is what leaders must build: not isolated events, but a visible pathway from contact to closeness, from closeness to rootedness, and from rootedness to responsibility.

## Patterns and Anti-Patterns

### The Pattern

A healthy pattern is easy to recognize. There is a clear first step. There is named follow-up. People move from event to table. Younger believers are brought close enough to watch and practice. Responsibility is entrusted before polish, with oversight still in place.

### The Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is easy to recognize too. The event is strong, but the follow-up is weak. There are plenty of volunteers, but almost no shepherding. Healing has no next step. Students are gathered but not known. Younger leaders are used for energy but never trained. Seniors are cared for but never entrusted. There is plenty of talk about disciple-making, but almost no visible path for learning how.

## Leadership That Sends

Biblical leadership is never about staying necessary forever. It is about making the body stronger and clearer. Leaders do not hoard ministry. They reproduce it.

So if you want to lead, lead with a framework people can actually use. Keep Engage, Establish, and Empower visible. Bring younger men close. Bring younger women close. Let them see how ministry works in kitchens, hallways, hospital rooms, youth nights, prayer gatherings, campuses, and homes. Do not only tell them to make disciples. Show them how. Then stay near enough to correct, strengthen, and send them.

This is how leadership serves renewal. Not by spectacle. Not by vague permission. Not by endlessly saying the right things from a distance. Renewal gains strength when leaders feed the sheep, establish the young, empower the faithful, and make the path plain enough to walk.

## Here Am I

All of this begins somewhere. Not with a strategy document or a leadership conference, but with a person who has stood before God and been undone.

Isaiah gives the right order. He sees the holiness of God, becomes aware of his uncleanness, receives cleansing, and then hears the call. Only then does he say, "Here am I. Send me."

That is still the order. Encounter. Repentance. Grace. Surrender. Then leadership.

So let the call remain concrete. Engage people. Establish believers. Empower the saints. Bring younger believers close enough to learn. Teach them the Word. Let them watch you. Let them do it with you. Then send them to do the same for others. That is leadership that serves the church, protects the framework, and helps a people become useful again in the hands of God.

## Questions for Reflection

Sit with these questions slowly in the presence of God. Do not answer them quickly.

1. Am I making the path of disciple-making clear, or am I only talking about it in general terms?
   Verses: Matthew 28:19-20; John 21:15-17; Ephesians 4:11-12

2. Who is close enough to my life to actually learn how I pray, follow up, serve, and disciple?
   Verses: Mark 3:14; 1 Corinthians 11:1; 2 Timothy 3:10-11

3. Am I establishing and empowering younger believers at the same time, or waiting so long that they never learn to carry responsibility?
   Verses: 2 Timothy 2:2; Titus 2:3-8; Luke 10:1-2

4. Where in our church are we running good events without a clear path from Engage to Establish to Empower?
   Verses: Acts 2:42-47; Colossians 1:28-29; Hebrews 10:24-25

5. What next step of responsibility do I need to entrust now so someone younger can watch, practice, and grow?
   Verses: Luke 9:1-2; 2 Timothy 2:2; 1 Peter 4:10
