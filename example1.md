# Example 1 - Perpetua and Felicity: Named by Christ in the Public Square

Before the church can carry mission faithfully, it has to know whose it is. Perpetua and Felicity answer that question before the church had buildings, influence, or public respectability. In Carthage in AD 203, they stood in a world ordered by rank, family expectation, and imperial religion, and they made it plain that their deepest identity was received from Christ, not from Rome. Their witness still matters wherever the church is relearning its identity.

## The World Around Them

Carthage was one of the great cities of Roman North Africa. It was prosperous, disciplined, and public. Honor mattered. Rank mattered. Loyalty to the civic order mattered. Religion was not treated as a private preference but as part of the fabric that held the city together. To refuse the empire's expectations was not merely to hold an unusual opinion. It was to look disloyal, disruptive, and dangerous.

Perpetua and Felicity came from very different places inside that order. Perpetua was young, well born, and still nursing her infant son. Felicity was enslaved and pregnant. In an ordinary week, the city would have kept such women far apart. But when they were arrested with other catechumens, prison placed them under the same sentence and the same Lord. Rome saw one free woman and one enslaved woman. The church learned to see two sisters.

## What They Did

The surviving account of Perpetua's imprisonment gives unusual closeness to the story. We do not meet an abstract idea of martyrdom. We meet a daughter whose father begged her to bend, a mother who feared for her child, a prisoner who knew the heat, crowding, and humiliation of jail, and a believer who had come to understand that Christ had the first claim on her life.

Her father pleaded with her repeatedly. He wanted his daughter back, her child protected, and the household spared disgrace. Yet when she stood before the tribunal, Perpetua answered with simple clarity: "I am a Christian." She would not let the empire, her family, or fear rename her.

Felicity bore the same witness from another angle. She was pregnant in prison and feared she would be kept back from martyrdom because Roman custom delayed the execution of pregnant women. The little band prayed, her labor came, and she gave birth only days before the games. When someone mocked her cries and asked how she would face the beasts if childbirth already overcame her, she answered that what she suffered then was her own pain, but in the arena she would suffer for Another. Even there, her life was interpreted through belonging to Christ.

On the day of the games, both women refused the symbolic humiliation of being dressed as pagan priestesses. In the arena they were wounded, exposed, and made into public spectacle, but they did not become the empire's possession. Perpetua still thought about modesty. Felicity still stood with the others. Near the end, Perpetua urged her companions to "stand fast in the faith." Their witness was not loud in the modern sense. It was clear, public, and unyielding.

## Legacy and Impact

Perpetua and Felicity did not build an institution, lead a movement, or hold office. That is precisely why their importance is easy to miss if the church measures significance only by visible influence. Their legacy was the legacy of identity made visible. Their story was copied, read, remembered, and handed down because the early church understood that public courage like this clarified the gospel for everyone else.

Their witness also exposed something the church would need to remember in every century. Christian identity does not erase earthly distinctions, but it does relativize them. A noblewoman and an enslaved woman stood together under one Lord. Their social location mattered, but it did not define them finally. They bore the image of God and the name of Christ in a way the empire could not revoke.

## What This Life Shows

Perpetua and Felicity make this burden concrete. Before believers can be sent, before churches can be renewed, before mission can become concrete, people must know who they are before God. If identity is vague, courage collapses. If identity is borrowed from the surrounding order, witness becomes thin. But if a person knows that he or she belongs to Christ, then even a hostile age cannot finally name that life.

This is what it means to be salt and light in a dark place. Not first visibility, strategy, or status, but a life so anchored in Christ that truth can be seen in public. Once that identity is settled, another question rises with force: what should a redeemed life now be spent doing?
