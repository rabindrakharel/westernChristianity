# Example 1 - Perpetua and Felicity: Identity Before the Arena

Before the church had public influence, it had to learn how to stand without it. Before Christians were associated with buildings, institutions, councils, and nations, they were often known by prison cells, broken households, and public shame. Perpetua and Felicity force the question beneath all easier questions: who are you when obedience costs you something immediate, visible, and irreversible?

## The World Around Them

Vibia Perpetua was about twenty-two, from a well-placed family, honorably married, and still nursing her infant son. Felicity was an enslaved woman in the same city, and she was still pregnant when the day of the games drew near. Their lives would ordinarily have moved along very different tracks. Carthage was ordered by rank, household, and public standing, and women like these were not expected to meet as equals. Yet in prison they were bound into the same little company of catechumens, and under the same sentence.

Carthage itself was one of Roman North Africa's great cities, prosperous from trade, agriculture, and imperial rule. Its port carried commerce, and the surrounding countryside fed its markets. Public life turned on rank, family reputation, civic ritual, and visible loyalty. Temples, festivals, and spectacles helped hold that order together. In a city like that, people knew where they stood, whom they were expected to honor, and how quickly suspicion could fall on anyone who refused the public order.

The world is older than our moment, but the pressure was familiar even then. Every age builds its own liturgies of approval. Every age offers safety to those who conform and suspicion to those who refuse. Rome had its altars, ceremonies, and imperial expectations. Our own age has its own rituals of belonging. In both, the question is similar: what name will you keep when the surrounding order offers safety in exchange for inward surrender?

In AD 203 they were arrested with Revocatus, Saturninus, and Secundulus while still catechumens, and a man named Saturus later gave himself up to join them. Perpetua's account survives in her own hand, one of the rare surviving texts written by a Christian woman in the ancient world. Through it we do not meet an idea of martyrdom, but a young mother, a grieving daughter, a prisoner, and a believer learning in real time what faithfulness would cost.

Rome saw the difference between Perpetua and Felicity immediately. Christ named them more deeply than Rome could. That is the point. Christian identity did not erase earthly circumstance, but it placed both women under a belonging deeper than class, status, family pressure, or fear of death.

## The Name They Would Not Trade

Perpetua and her companions were arrested during the persecution under Septimius Severus, when conversion itself had become dangerous. The pressure placed on them was simple and brutal. Renounce Christ, submit to the religious demands of the empire, and live. Refuse, and die in the arena.

Perpetua's father pleaded with her repeatedly. He wanted his daughter back, her child protected, and the household spared disgrace. The surviving account preserves the scene with painful clarity. He argued, wept, kissed her hands, threw himself at her feet, and begged her to think of her infant son. Earlier, after she pointed to an ordinary vessel and asked whether it could be called by another name, she told him she could call herself nothing else. Before the tribunal she spoke even more plainly: "I am a Christian."

The prison itself was no place of noble calm. Perpetua wrote of "a bitter day," of heat, crowding, rough soldiers, and the misery of seeing her nursing child suffer. Deacons bribed the guards so the prisoners could be moved for a few hours to a better part of the jail. Her baby was brought to her. She suckled him there. For a moment the prison, she said, became "a palace" because care, fellowship, and Christ's presence entered the darkness.

But Perpetua understood that the demand being made of her was not merely tactical. It was about identity. She could not preserve her life by denying the Lord to whom her life belonged. She had come to understand herself first as Christ's. That did not make her cold toward her father or careless toward her child. It made her clear. She knew that if she let the empire rename her, she would not actually be saving herself. She would be abandoning herself.

Felicity's witness sharpened the same truth from another angle. She was pregnant in prison and feared she would be kept back from martyrdom and separated from the others because Roman custom delayed the execution of pregnant women. Three days before the games, the little band prayed together, and her labor came. When someone mocked her cries and asked how she would face the beasts if she could barely bear childbirth, she answered with grave simplicity that now she suffered for herself, but there would be Another in her then, because she would be suffering for Him. She gave birth to a daughter, and another Christian woman took the child to raise.

As the day of the games arrived, the pressure became more public and more theatrical. The crowd wanted spectacle. The authorities wanted submission. Perpetua and Felicity refused even the symbolic humiliation of being dressed as pagan priestesses. In the arena they were thrown to a savage cow. Perpetua fell, rose, pulled her torn robe over her thigh, and pinned up her hair, caring more for modesty than pain. After the attack, dazed and bloodied, she seemed almost to wake from a vision and asked when they were to face the beast. Near the end she gathered enough clarity to tell the others, "Stand fast in the faith," and to urge them to love one another.

That is why their witness still carries force. One was free, one was enslaved. One was socially elevated, one was not. Yet in the decisive hour both stood as sisters. Christian identity was not a private sentiment added on top of ordinary life. It was the deepest truth about who they were.

## Life Was Precious, But Not Ultimate

The courage of martyrs is often misunderstood. They are not people who despise life. They are people who have learned to rank it properly. Perpetua loved her child. Felicity felt labor pains. These women were not detached from earthly affection. They were not performing drama for admiration. They simply knew that life under God is larger than physical survival.

That clarity gave them unusual steadiness. They understood that the body can be harmed and still belong to God. They understood that earthly life is temporary, but not meaningless. Because it is temporary, it must not be spent in denial of the One who gives it. The brevity of life did not make obedience less urgent. It made obedience more urgent.

This is one of the great recoveries the modern church needs. Many people treat mortality either as a terror to be avoided at all costs or as a vague abstraction to be ignored until it forces itself into view. The early martyrs handled it differently. They faced death in the light of resurrection. That did not remove pain, but it removed death's claim to final authority. The empire could end their days. It could not define their future.

## Identity Stronger Than Power

Perpetua and Felicity expose how shallow many modern identity claims really are. The surrounding culture tells people to construct themselves from desire, status, tribe, achievement, or feeling. Rome did something similar with different symbols. It offered belonging on imperial terms. These women refused. Their lives say, with terrible clarity, that identity received from Christ is stronger than identity imposed by power.

A heavenly calling does not begin when someone is famous, educated, publicly useful, or strategically placed. It begins when a person knows to whom he or she belongs. From there courage becomes possible. A mother in prison can bear witness. An enslaved woman in pain can endure with dignity. The decisive issue is not social leverage. It is spiritual allegiance.

For the church in the West, this story is not mainly a summons to admire heroic women from long ago. It is a summons to recover the same order of loves. If Christ is Lord, then comfort cannot be lord. If resurrection is true, then fear cannot rule the soul. If life is temporary, then it must be spent faithfully rather than merely preserved anxiously.

This is where renewal begins. A people who know who they are can endure loss without collapse. A people who know that life is brief can spend it well. And once that identity is clear, the question shifts to ordinary places, among less famous people, on the margins of history. From Carthage, the story turns to Roman Britain and to a man named Alban.
