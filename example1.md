# Example 1 - Perpetua and Felicity: Identity Before the Arena

Before the church had public influence, it had to learn how to stand without it. Before Christians were associated with buildings, institutions, councils, and nations, they were often known by prison cells, broken households, and public shame. That is why this sequence begins with Perpetua and Felicity. Their story forces the question beneath all easier questions: who are you when obedience costs you something immediate, visible, and irreversible?

## The World Around Them

To feel the force of their witness, the reader has to enter Carthage for a moment. This was not a forgotten village on the empire's edge. It was one of Roman North Africa's great cities, wealthy from trade, agriculture, and imperial order. It was socially stratified, publicly performative, and saturated with status. Family honor mattered. Civic religion mattered. Public festivals and spectacles mattered. To belong in such a place was to know your rank, respect the gods that guarded public life, and avoid any loyalty that made you look dangerous, disloyal, or strange.

That climate is not as distant from the modern reader as it first appears. The symbols have changed, but the pressure feels familiar. People still live under systems that reward conformity, punish public deviance, and ask individuals to preserve comfort, family reputation, and social belonging above truth. Rome had its altars, ceremonies, and imperial expectations. Modern life has its own liturgies of approval. In both cases the question is similar: what name will you keep when the surrounding world offers safety in exchange for inward surrender?

In Carthage in AD 203, two women stood under the same sentence. Perpetua was a young mother from a prosperous family. Felicity was an enslaved woman and was pregnant while imprisoned. Rome saw their difference in rank. Christ named them with the same name. That is the point. Christian identity did not erase earthly circumstance, but it placed both women under a deeper belonging than class, status, family pressure, or fear of death.

## The Name They Would Not Trade

Perpetua and her companions were arrested as new believers during the persecution under Septimius Severus. The pressure placed on them was simple and brutal. Renounce Christ, submit to the religious demands of the empire, and live. Refuse, and die in the arena. Perpetua's father pleaded with her repeatedly. He wanted his daughter back, her child protected, and the household spared disgrace. Those are not small pressures. They are the kinds of pressures that bend many people even now, though usually in quieter forms.

But Perpetua understood that the demand being made of her was not merely tactical. It was about identity. She could not preserve her life by denying the Lord to whom her life belonged. She had come to understand herself first as Christ's. That did not make her cold toward her father or careless toward her child. It made her clear. She knew that if she let the empire rename her, she would not actually be saving herself. She would be abandoning herself.

Felicity's witness sharpened the same truth from another angle. She was pregnant in prison and gave birth shortly before her execution. Her body was under strain, her social position was low, and her future in earthly terms was almost gone. Yet her suffering did not strip her of dignity. In Christ she was not reduced to her status as a slave or her vulnerability as a woman awaiting childbirth in chains. She belonged to the kingdom that Rome could not master.

That is why their witness still carries force. One was free, one was enslaved. One was socially elevated, one was not. Yet in the decisive hour both stood as sisters. Christian identity was not a private sentiment added on top of ordinary life. It was the deepest truth about who they were.

## Life Was Precious, But Not Ultimate

The courage of martyrs is often misunderstood. They are not people who despise life. They are people who have learned to rank it properly. Perpetua loved her child. Felicity felt labor pains. These women were not detached from earthly affection. They were not performing drama for admiration. They simply knew that life under God is larger than physical survival.

That clarity gave them unusual steadiness. They understood that the body can be harmed and still belong to God. They understood that earthly life is temporary, but not meaningless. Because it is temporary, it must not be spent in denial of the One who gives it. The brevity of life did not make obedience less urgent. It made obedience more urgent.

This is one of the great recoveries the modern church needs. Many people treat mortality either as a terror to be avoided at all costs or as a vague abstraction to be ignored until it forces itself into view. The early martyrs handled it differently. They faced death in the light of resurrection. That did not remove pain, but it removed death's claim to final authority. The empire could end their days. It could not define their future.

## Why Their Story Belongs Near the Question of Identity

Perpetua and Felicity belong near Chapter 1 of this manuscript because they expose how shallow many modern identity claims really are. The surrounding culture tells people to construct themselves from desire, status, tribe, achievement, or feeling. Rome did something similar with different symbols. It offered belonging on imperial terms. These women refused. Their lives say, with terrible clarity, that identity received from Christ is stronger than identity imposed by power.

They also belong near the question of calling. A heavenly calling does not begin when someone is famous, educated, publicly useful, or strategically placed. It begins when a person knows to whom he or she belongs. From there courage becomes possible. A mother in prison can bear witness. An enslaved woman in pain can endure with dignity. The decisive issue is not social leverage. It is spiritual allegiance.

For the church in the West, this story is not mainly a summons to admire heroic women from long ago. It is a summons to recover the same order of loves. If Christ is Lord, then comfort cannot be lord. If resurrection is true, then fear cannot rule the soul. If life is temporary, then it must be spent faithfully rather than merely preserved anxiously.

This is where renewal begins. A people who know who they are can endure loss without collapse. A people who know that life is brief can spend it well. And once that identity is clear, the next question follows naturally: what does obedience look like in ordinary places, among less famous people, on the margins of history? That is where the next example takes us, from Carthage to Roman Britain, and to a man named Alban.
