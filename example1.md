# Example 1 - Perpetua and Felicity: Identity Before the Arena

In AD 203, Christians in Carthage were not a respected public presence. They were a small and often suspect people, known less for buildings or influence than for prison cells, household strain, and the shame that could follow public confession. Perpetua and Felicity stand at that point of pressure, and their story presses a question that still reaches beneath easier ones: who are you when obedience costs something immediate, visible, and irreversible?

## The World Around Them

Vibia Perpetua was about twenty-two, from a well-placed family, honorably married, and still nursing her infant son. Felicity was an enslaved woman in the same city, and she was still pregnant when the day of the games drew near. Their lives would ordinarily have moved along very different tracks. Carthage was ordered by rank, household, and public standing, and women like these were not expected to meet as equals. Yet in prison they were bound into the same little company of catechumens, and under the same sentence.

Carthage itself was one of Roman North Africa's great cities, prosperous from trade, agriculture, and imperial rule. Its port carried commerce, and the surrounding countryside fed its markets. Public life turned on rank, family reputation, civic ritual, and visible loyalty. Temples, festivals, and spectacles helped hold that order together. In a city like that, people knew where they stood, whom they were expected to honor, and how quickly suspicion could fall on anyone who refused the public order.

The forms change from age to age, but the pressure is recognizable. Rome tied public loyalty to altars, ceremonies, and imperial expectation. Modern societies use different symbols, yet they also reward conformity and mark dissent as dangerous or strange. The same question returns in every age: what name will you keep when belonging is offered on terms that ask for inward surrender?

In AD 203 they were arrested with Revocatus, Saturninus, and Secundulus while still catechumens, and a man named Saturus later gave himself up to join them. Perpetua's account survives in her own hand, one of the rare surviving texts written by a Christian woman in the ancient world. Through it we do not meet an idea of martyrdom, but a young mother, a grieving daughter, a prisoner, and a believer learning in real time what faithfulness would cost.

Rome sorted them according to rank, but the church received them as sisters. Christian identity did not erase earthly circumstance, but it placed both women under a belonging deeper than class, household, public standing, or fear of death.

## The Name They Would Not Trade

Perpetua and her companions were arrested during the persecution under Septimius Severus, when conversion itself had become dangerous. The demand before them was brutally plain: submit to the religious claims of the empire and live, or refuse and die in the arena.

Perpetua's father pleaded with her repeatedly. He wanted his daughter back, her child protected, and the household spared disgrace. The surviving account preserves the scene with painful clarity. He argued, wept, kissed her hands, threw himself at her feet, and begged her to think of her infant son. Earlier, after she pointed to an ordinary vessel and asked whether it could be called by another name, she told him she could call herself nothing else. Before the tribunal she spoke even more plainly: "I am a Christian."

The prison itself was no place of noble calm. Perpetua wrote of "a bitter day," of heat, crowding, rough soldiers, and the misery of seeing her nursing child suffer. Deacons bribed the guards so the prisoners could be moved for a few hours to a better part of the jail. Her baby was brought to her. She suckled him there. For a moment the prison, she said, became "a palace" because care, fellowship, and Christ's presence entered the darkness.

Perpetua understood that the demand before her was not merely political or tactical. It reached into the center of who she was. To deny Christ would not have been a strategic concession that left the rest of her untouched; it would have meant speaking falsely about the Lord to whom her life belonged. That clarity did not make her cold toward her father or careless toward her child. It made her steady in the middle of grief.

Felicity's witness sharpened the same truth from another angle. She was pregnant in prison and feared she would be kept back from martyrdom and separated from the others because Roman custom delayed the execution of pregnant women. Three days before the games, the little band prayed together, and her labor came. When someone mocked her cries and asked how she would face the beasts if she could barely bear childbirth, she answered with grave simplicity that now she suffered for herself, but there would be Another in her then, because she would be suffering for Him. She gave birth to a daughter, and another Christian woman took the child to raise.

When the day of the games came, the pressure widened into public spectacle. The crowd came for blood and novelty, and the authorities meant to display submission as much as punishment. Perpetua and Felicity refused even the symbolic humiliation of being dressed as pagan priestesses. In the arena they were thrown before a wild cow. Perpetua fell, rose, drew her torn robe over her thigh, and pinned up her loosened hair, more concerned for modesty than for display. After the attack, still dazed, she seemed almost to wake from a vision and asked when they were to face the beast. Near the end she told the others, "Stand fast in the faith," and urged them to love one another.

One woman came from a respected household and one from slavery, yet in the decisive hour they stood as sisters. Christian identity was not a private sentiment laid over ordinary life. It named their deepest allegiance and held when every other claim pressed against it.

## Life Was Precious, But Not Ultimate

The courage of martyrs is often misunderstood. They are sometimes imagined as people who despise earthly life, but Perpetua's nursing son and Felicity's labor pains prevent that mistake. These women were not detached from affection or indifferent to pain. They had learned to place earthly life beneath the Lord who gave it.

That ordering gave them steadiness. They knew the body could be harmed and still belong to God. They knew earthly life was temporary, but not meaningless, and for that very reason it could not be spent denying the One who gives it. The brevity of life did not make obedience less urgent. It made obedience more urgent.

The modern church needs that recovery. Many people treat mortality either as a terror to be avoided at all costs or as a haze to be ignored until it forces itself into view. The early martyrs faced it differently. They met death in the light of resurrection. That did not remove pain, but it stripped death of final authority. The empire could end their days. It could not define their future.

## Identity Stronger Than Power

Perpetua and Felicity expose how thin many modern identity claims are. The surrounding culture tells people to construct themselves from desire, status, tribe, achievement, or feeling. Rome offered the same bargain in different symbols: belong on imperial terms and keep your place. These women refused, and their witness still shows that identity received from Christ can outlast identity imposed by power.

A heavenly calling does not begin when someone becomes famous, educated, publicly useful, or strategically placed. It begins when a person knows to whom he or she belongs. From there courage becomes possible. A mother in prison can bear witness. An enslaved woman in pain can endure with dignity. Social leverage is not the decisive issue; spiritual allegiance is.

For the church in the West, this story is not mainly a reason to admire heroic women from long ago. It is a summons to recover the same order of loves. If Christ is Lord, comfort cannot quietly take His place. If resurrection is true, fear cannot be allowed to govern the soul. If life is temporary, then it must be spent faithfully rather than anxiously preserved.

This is where renewal begins. A people who know who they are can endure loss without collapse and spend their brief lives well. Once that identity is clear, the question shifts to ordinary places, among less famous people, on the margins of history. From Carthage, the story turns to Roman Britain and to a man named Alban.
