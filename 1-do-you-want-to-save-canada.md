Chapter 1 — Do You Want to Save Canada?

There are moments when a nation can feel like a house with good paint and cracked foundations. The lights are on, the streets are busy, and yet underneath the noise there is a quiet tremor: anxiety in families, distrust in institutions, loneliness in neighborhoods, confusion in classrooms, and exhaustion in churches.

Canada is living in such a moment.

You can feel it at the dinner table when children ask hard questions that parents aren’t sure how to answer. You can feel it when addiction steals another son, when violence takes another life, when public discourse becomes more hostile and less truthful, and when church members quietly disappear without anyone noticing for weeks.

Canada has not collapsed. It remains a beautiful nation filled with generous people and real opportunity.

And still—something beneath the surface feels fragile.

So the question is not dramatic. It is necessary:

Do you want to save Canada?

But the moment we ask that question, we have to purify what we mean by save.

Do we mean returning to comfort, winning cultural arguments, defeating ideological opponents, protecting our tribe, and restoring a version of the country that made us feel safe?

Or do we mean something more biblical and more costly: a people renewed by repentance, communities healed by truth and mercy, and churches re-formed for faithful witness?

The gospel does not permit shallow patriotism or despairing cynicism. It calls us to honest diagnosis, surrendered hearts, and courageous obedience.

Love That Refuses to Look Away

Do you love your country?

Not in the shallow way that appears for a moment during a hockey game or a national holiday. Not the kind of love that waves a flag once a year and then returns to ordinary life.

I mean something deeper.

Do you love your country enough to be troubled by what is happening to it?

If you pay attention long enough, you will notice that the unease is not merely political or economic.

It is spiritual.

Young people are not primarily asking about tax brackets or party platforms. They are asking questions closer to the bone:

Does my life matter?
Why am I here?
What is the point of all of this?

Those questions do not emerge from a policy dispute.

They emerge from a meaning crisis.

And when a society begins to lose its sense of meaning, everything else begins to shake.

From Alarm and Blame to Lament and Holy Responsibility

Most citizens move through a predictable emotional cycle in times of decline. It often begins with Alarm (“How did this happen so quickly?”), quickly hardens into Blame (“Who ruined the country?”), wears down into Fatigue (“I can’t keep caring this much.”), and eventually settles into Cynicism (“Nothing is real; nothing will change.”).

The church is not immune to this same progression.

We can sound prophetic while becoming reactionary.
We can sound principled while becoming harsh.
We can speak about hope while quietly surrendering to resignation.

Scripture gives us a better path.

Instead of denial, we learn Lament.
Instead of accusation, we practice Repentance.
Instead of panic, we pursue Discernment.
Instead of passivity, we embrace Responsibility.

Lament tells the truth about pain.
Repentance tells the truth about us.
Discernment tells the truth about causes.
Responsibility tells the truth about calling.

This is the third response—beyond avoidance and overwhelm.

Not retreat. Not rage.

Holy responsibility.

Ground Truth: What We Are Actually Facing

Canada’s crisis is not reducible to one policy or one party. It is deeper and more formative, and it is showing up in everyday life:

Families are increasingly fragmented and overburdened.
Youth are formed more by digital ecosystems than by trusted adults.
Moral vocabulary is loud, but moral formation is thin.
Mental and relational distress is widespread and often untreated.
Churches are busy, yet many believers remain under-discipled.

Public confusion is often the visible fruit of long private erosion.

And if we only treat symptoms, we will remain trapped in reaction cycles.

Real renewal requires root-level discipleship.

Why Nations Drift Before They Collapse

Nations usually do not collapse all at once. They drift. They erode. They hollow out from the inside.

Here are five root patterns that show up long before the headlines become alarming:

1) Formation gaps in the primary places of discipleship

Home and church were meant to be foundational sites of formation. In many places, they became reactive rather than intentionally formative.

2) Competing catechisms shaping identity every day

Algorithms, entertainment, and ideological tribes disciple imagination continuously. If the church disciples weekly while digital systems disciple hourly, the outcome is predictable.

3) Moral language without moral architecture

A society can talk constantly about rights and justice while losing shared commitments to truth, restraint, covenant loyalty, and accountability.

4) Public performance replacing public virtue

Visibility is rewarded more than integrity. Outrage receives more attention than wisdom. Image often outpaces character.

5) Unhealed wounds becoming social instability

Addiction, fatherlessness, unresolved trauma, economic stress, and relational abandonment eventually become public patterns: crime, alienation, distrust, and despair.

That is the deeper story.

Not just politics.

Formation.

Designed for More Than Routine

Human beings were not created merely to survive routines.

Animals eat, sleep, migrate, reproduce, and care for their young. Their lives revolve around instinct and survival.

Humans share some of those rhythms—work, family, rest.

But something inside us refuses to accept that this is all there is.

Every person carries a quiet longing to be part of something larger than themselves:

A purpose.
A mission.
A calling.

Scripture explains why.

From the beginning, God declares that humanity is created in His image—and immediately gives a commission:

“Be fruitful and multiply. Fill the earth and subdue it.” (Genesis 1:26–28)

Humans were never meant to be passive observers of the world. We were created for image-bearing stewardship—cultivating, building, ordering, blessing.

Then God calls Abraham outward:

“Go… and through you all the families of the earth will be blessed.” (Genesis 12:1–3)

And Jesus compresses the whole moral life into two commands:

Love God fully.
Love your neighbor truly.

Finally, Jesus speaks the mission that defines the church:

“All authority… has been given to Me… Go and make disciples of all nations.” (Matthew 28:18–20)

The biblical storyline gives both diagnosis and destiny.

Christian hope is not nostalgia for a mythic past. It is confidence in a living King who still transforms people, restores communities, and sustains faithful witness in hard times.

Scriptural hope is also honest: renewal is usually slow, costly, and contested.

But it is real.

Salt and Light in a Decaying World

Jesus used two metaphors that leave no room for passive faith:

“You are the salt of the earth.”
“You are the light of the world.”

Salt preserves what decays.

Light exposes what hides.

Notice: Jesus did not say believers should try to become salt and light. He said they are.

Which raises an uncomfortable question:

If the environment is decaying, where is the salt?

If darkness is spreading, where is the light?

It is easy to blame politicians. It is easy to blame immigrants or institutions. It is easy to blame ideology, culture, or the latest headline villain.

Blaming is easy.

But Scripture gives a harder word:

“Judgment begins in the house of God.”

Not as condemnation, but as mercy.

God confronts what He intends to heal.

Before we diagnose Canada, we must examine the church in Canada.

Have we substituted activity for formation?
Have we preferred safety over truth-telling love?
Have we delegated discipleship to specialists?
Have we preached mission but practiced maintenance?

The question becomes personal.

Here Am I

Throughout Scripture, God’s call is not answered by perfect people. It is answered by available people.

Isaiah’s response still echoes:

“Here am I. Send me.”

That response does not require fame or extraordinary skill. It begins with something smaller:

A willingness to care.
A willingness to notice.
A willingness to step into the brokenness rather than step around it.

This is where “saving Canada” becomes clear.

Not saving the nation through cultural dominance.

Not saving it through outrage.

Not saving it through nostalgia.

But through a church that can weep, repent, disciple, and serve.

Because transformation rarely begins with crowds.

It begins with one life at a time.

One person shares the gospel with one friend.

One family opens their home to neighbors.

One student introduces Christ to classmates.

One conversation becomes a lifelong journey of discipleship.

A father encounters Christ—and his household changes.

A young adult discovers purpose—and their future becomes a platform for blessing.

Refusing False Saviors

The crisis we are in will tempt us toward false saviors.

Outrage feels like action, but it often produces heat without healing.
Ideology feels like clarity, but it often reshapes the church into a tribe.
Nostalgia feels comforting, but it cannot disciple a new generation.
Withdrawal feels safe, but it abandons the mission field.

If we refuse false saviors, we can recover a biblical posture:

Name reality without panic.
Refuse shallow patriotism and despairing cynicism.
Recover lament, repentance, and discernment.
Return to discipleship roots in households, churches, and local communities.
Re-enter public witness with truth, humility, and sacrificial love.

This book is written to help the church do exactly that.

The Battlefield of Meaning

If you want to understand the future of a nation, pay attention to its universities and schools.

Every politician, teacher, journalist, entrepreneur, and civic leader passes through those institutions at some point. And young people arrive there hungry—not merely for information, but for identity.

They are hungry for meaning.

Hungry for belonging.

Hungry for a story big enough to explain their suffering, their ambition, their relationships, their fears.

If the church disciples weekly while digital systems disciple hourly, the outcome is predictable.

Someone will disciple them.

If the church is absent, something else will fill the vacuum.

Because human beings will always give themselves to a story that promises purpose.

The question is never whether they will follow a story.

The question is which story they will follow.

Where the Story Begins

Imagine two rooms.

In one room, older believers sit together discussing the Book of Revelation. They debate timelines, symbols, and the return of Christ. Their conversation is sincere. They love Jesus. They long for His appearing.

In another room, university students sit with friends asking quieter questions:

Why do I feel lost?
Does my life matter?
What is the purpose of everything?

Two generations.

Two conversations.

Both searching.

And somewhere between those rooms lies the future of the church.

Because when those conversations meet, something remarkable happens.

The wisdom of experience meets the hunger for meaning.
Eschatology meets anxiety.
Prophecy meets purpose.
And the gospel becomes not only a future hope, but a present mission.

So where will those conversations meet?

Not primarily in a program.

Not first in a polished event.

But in the place where people can be seen, known, challenged, and loved.

Around a table.
